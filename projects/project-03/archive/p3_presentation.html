Before — The Friction

2 Hours, 30 Tabs, Wrong Size

A real shoe-shopping story. 847 Amazon results for "boys winter PE shoes." 12 tabs open. A comparison table built in Google Docs. 3 different sites cross-referenced. Called your son over to look at the tiny phone screen. Ordered. Wrong size. Return.

01

Qualitative Constraints Can't Filter

"Not fluffy" doesn't exist in product specs. It lives only in reviews — buried in natural language, never structured as a filterable attribute.

02

Cross-Site Comparison is Manual Labor

Amazon, Zappos, Nike — pasted into a Google Docs table. The user becomes their own comparison agent, building disposable infrastructure.

03

Size Varies Between Brands

"Size 3" means different things at Nike vs Adidas vs New Balance. No system normalizes this. Every purchase is a gamble.

04

Phone Screen Breaks Family Decisions

Tiny screen, show one product at a time, can't compare side-by-side. The child can't meaningfully participate in the choice.

05

Decision Dynamics Get Distorted

Child sees prices → picks the most expensive one → parent feels guilt. The information architecture itself creates friction.

"Behind every workaround is a friction point. Building a comparison table in Google Docs reveals a structural role the system is missing."

— Article #05, Friction Discovery

Translation Layer

From Human Words to System Parameters

"Not fluffy" doesn't appear in any product name or spec sheet. It lives only in reviews. The Translation Layer is the missing bridge — turning qualitative human constraints into machine-readable specifications.

Step 1

Semantic Decomposition

"Fluffy" = fleece-lined, sherpa, fur, plush lining. The system expands a single human word into a cluster of product-domain synonyms.

Step 2

Multi-Modal Verification

NLP on 300+ reviews + product image analysis + spec cross-reference. Three independent signals triangulate the truth.

Step 3

Confidence Scoring

Candidate A: 94%. Candidate B: 88%. Candidate C: 71% — below threshold, triggers escalation. The user sees certainty, not guesswork.

Core Thesis

The Translation Layer is Article #01's core thesis fully implemented: translating human intent into system specification. Every qualitative constraint a person voices — "not fluffy," "cool-looking," "easy to put on" — becomes a structured query the system can act on.

Human-Human Facilitation

AI Protects the Family Moment

From Article #11 (Ren — 仁): AI doesn't make the decision. It creates an environment where the family can decide together — with joy. The system's role is facilitation, not automation.

Principle 1

Information Architecture

TV = child-friendly (emotional features, big images). Phone = parent-only (price, confidence, buy). Each screen serves a different participant's needs.

Principle 2

Participation Balance

Child's preference = top priority (point to choose). Parent's constraints = protected behind the scenes. Both voices matter, neither dominates.

Principle 3

Dynamic Protection

Price hidden from TV = prevents "I want the expensive one!" dynamic. 3 candidates only = eliminates choice paralysis. Architecture shapes behavior.

AI ~25%
Human ~75%

AI handles filtering, verification, and surface optimization. Humans handle choosing, judging, and conversation.
