Why This Works

All 8 Context Tokens fired across 4 phases.

6 tokens changed state as the user moved from station → train → living room. Each change reshaped the interface.

01Intent
What the user needs to accomplish right now
Shifted from Explore (station/train) to Decide (living room). The same task, but the system's role changed — from research assistant to family facilitator.
02Physical State
What the body can physically do
Both hands (station) → One hand (train) → Both hands (living room). Touch targets, interaction zones, and information density all adapted per phase.
03Cognitive Load
Available mental bandwidth
Moderate throughout — not depleted like P2. But living room cognitive mode shifts to family mode: less analytical, more social and emotional.
04Social Exposure
Who can see the screen
Private → Public → Family. The biggest transformation. Family mode splits the information architecture: emotional content on TV, financial content on phone. Price is hidden from the child to protect Social Job.
05Priority Weight
When values conflict, which one wins
Child preference > Brand > Price. The priority order stays constant, but the UI enforces it — by hiding price from the person whose preference should come first.
06Synchro Rate
How much autonomy the AI gets
Delegate (search, filter, cross-reference) → Own (final purchase). AI reduces 847 to 3. Family picks the shoe. 3-step confirmation: remote → OK → Buy.
07Form Factor
What device is available right now
Phone only → Phone + TV. The most dramatic transformation in the portfolio. Phone stops being a display and becomes a controller. Product images are purged; remote appears.
08Feasibility
What is actually possible right now
In-stock only. Size 3 verified across brands. Delivery before weekend. Infeasible candidates never enter the pipeline — no "out of stock" surprises like last time.

Design decision highlights
Why hide the price from the TV?
When a child sees "$89" next to a shoe, they either pick the most expensive one (making the parent uncomfortable) or the cheapest (feeling guilty). Hiding price protects the Social Job: "We chose this together." The parent confirms on their phone — privately.
Rejected: show price on both screens for "transparency"
Why did the phone's product images disappear?
When the TV shows large shoe images, the phone doesn't need them. A remote control needs screen space. Keeping small duplicates would split attention between two screens — cognitive overhead with no benefit.
Rejected: keep thumbnails on phone alongside remote controls
Why not Slide-to-Buy in the living room?
The user is seated, stable, in a calm environment. remote → OK → Buy tap is 3 intentional steps — enough friction to confirm intent. Slide-to-Buy adds unnecessary weight to a low-risk moment.
Rejected: Slide-to-Buy for all purchase confirmations
