Origin

Why Context Grammar exists

I spent years designing interfaces for enterprise products — the kind that millions of people use every day without thinking about who designed them. I worked across platforms, devices, teams. I saw what good design could do at scale.

But I also kept running into the same wall. We had access to incredible data — location, schedule, device telemetry, search history. And yet the products we built still couldn't answer the simplest question: what does this person actually need right now?

AI keeps getting smarter at processing our words. It hasn't gotten any better at understanding our world.

That gap bothered me. Not the technology gap — the conceptual gap. We didn't have a shared vocabulary for human context. Designers would say "the user is stressed." Engineers would ask "what's the data point for stressed?" And nobody had an answer.

So I started building one. I began cataloguing the invisible forces that shape what people need from technology at any given moment — their physical state, their cognitive load, who's watching, what's at stake, how much time they have. And I found that these forces could be structured. Named. Measured. Passed between systems.

That's Context Grammar. Eight tokens that give AI a structured way to understand not just what someone said, but who they are when they said it.

Vision

This started as a framework.
It's becoming a design language.

The goal: a shared vocabulary that lets designers, engineers, and AI systems talk about human context in a structured, composable way.

Why now

Every major AI platform is converging on the same realization: context is the last mile. Models can generate, reason, and converse. What they can't do is understand the human on the other side. That's the gap Context Grammar fills.

What's next

Context Grammar is open. The framework, the token specifications, the research — all published here. The next step is applied work: real prototypes, real products, and a community of designers and engineers building with it.
