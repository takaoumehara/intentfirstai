Trust Architecture

AIとの信頼を設計する。段階的に、双方向で。

Why It Matters

AI is capable.
But you can't delegate yet.

ChatGPT can write. Gemini can plan your trip. But you don't want either of them spending your credit card without asking. Why? Because trust isn't there yet — and today's AI products have no architecture for building it.

  • AI systems have no mechanism to earn autonomy over time. Every session starts from zero.
  • Information sharing is designed only one way — users protect data from AI. The inverse direction (AI bridging human relationships) has no design language.
  • When AI makes a mistake, there is no graceful recovery path. Trust collapses silently.
  • Multi-person contexts (families, teams) require different trust levels per person, per domain, simultaneously — no platform addresses this.
The question isn't "what can AI do?" It's "what are you willing to let AI do?" — and that answer changes over time.

Context Grammar's answer: Trust is not a toggle. It is an architecture — with temporal stages, bidirectional flows, and graceful failure modes. These three principles make it designable.

The Framework

Three Principles of Trust Design

Context Grammar designs trust through three interlocking principles. Each addresses a different failure mode in today's AI.

Principle 01

Temporal Arc

Trust builds over time through accumulated results — not through configuration. Every AI relationship passes through five phases: Encounter, Learning, Maturation, Crisis, Ambient. Trying to skip a phase breaks the contract.

Trust climbs stairs. It falls by elevator.

Principle 02

Bidirectional Disclosure

Information sharing has two directions that must both be designed. The Protective direction controls what you share with AI. The Connective direction enables AI to bridge interests between people — a direction that exists in zero products today.

Principle 03

Graceful Demotion

When AI makes a mistake, the Autonomy Dial drops instantly — multiple levels at once. The human regains control. Recovery is gradual, transparent, and asymmetric: building trust takes months, losing it takes seconds.

Design for the fall before the rise.

The coupling rule: Disclosure ≥ Autonomy. You can't delegate what you haven't disclosed. Context Grammar enforces this structurally — the system won't advance Autonomy beyond what Disclosure supports.

A single failure triggers instant demotion — the dial drops multiple levels at once, at any phase.

Suggest — AI presents options; human decides everything. No assumptions.
Confirm — AI proposes based on learned patterns; human approves before execution.
Notify — AI acts and informs; human can override but doesn't need to initiate.
Auto — AI acts silently; human intervenes only if something is wrong.

The timeline is different for every domain. Music might reach Auto in a week. Healthcare might never get past Confirm. Trust isn't uniform — it's calibrated.

Think about becoming a regular at a restaurant. It takes six months before they bring your usual beer without asking. AI relationships follow the same arc.
Principle 02

Bidirectional Disclosure

Most AI products design only one information flow — what the user shares with AI. Context Grammar designs three distinct flows, each requiring different granularity and permissions.

Flow Direction What It Designs Example
Protective
User → AI 		Inbound Per-domain control over what data you share with AI services. Four levels: Full, Summary, Existence, Hidden. Health data: Full to medical AI, Hidden from hobby AI
Connective
AI → Human 		Bridging AI surfaces relevant signals between people at appropriate granularity — interests, habits, activities. Exists in zero products today. Son's Minecraft BGM: appears in Dad's Spotify playlist
Selective
AI ↔ Others 		Outbound Controlled disclosure to third parties. Based on intent, the AI decides what to share, with whom, and when. Medical info: Full to Mom, Summary to Dad, Hidden from school

The Connective direction is the gap. Spotify Family Mix blends randomly. Context Grammar's Connective flow bridges with context — "the song your son plays every day in Minecraft, placed in Dad's playlist." Discovery with intent, not coincidence.

Protective vs. Connective: Protective is "who do you tell about your allergies?" Connective is "recommend this new dish to the customer who loved the last one." Same act of sharing. Completely different purpose.

Principle 03

Graceful Demotion

The moment AI gets it wrong will come. A nut-allergy sufferer is served the wrong dish. An auto-order arrives for the wrong brand. The problem isn't the failure itself — it's what happens next.

Context Grammar's recovery path follows three rules:

  • Instant Retreat — The moment failure occurs, the related Autonomy Dial drops two levels immediately. Decision-making authority returns to the human.
  • Accountability — The system explains what went wrong. No black boxes. "A new staff member didn't check the allergy notes." Honest and specific.
  • Asymmetric Recovery — Rebuilding trust takes longer than the original trust-building. Not a restart from zero, but a more cautious re-entry. There are no shortcuts.
Building trust takes months.
Losing it takes seconds.
Designing for this asymmetry is the core of Trust Architecture.
