Context Grammar — Floor 1

Intent

Before AI reads the room, it must read what the user wants. Sometimes it's spoken. Often it isn't. Intent is the signal that starts every interaction — and the one today's AI almost always misses.

Intent is Floor 1 of Context Grammar. It's what the user wants in the moment — not a permanent goal, not a demographic. Four types: one stated, three inferred. Get Intent wrong and every floor above it fails.

Why Intent?

The gap between asked and wanted.

Samsung's Family Hub fridge knows your dietary restrictions. It knows every member of your household. It can read the barcode on the yogurt and recognize your voice. It is, in the strictest sense, a smart fridge.

And yet — at 7am on a Wednesday, when you're late and rushing, it cannot tell the difference between "what can I make for lunch?" (asked while grabbing a coffee, no time) and "what should I cook tonight?" (asked on Sunday morning, all the time in the world).

AI today is fluent in what you ask. It is deaf to what you want.

That deafness is Floor 1 of Context Grammar: a structured way for AI to recognize Intent — not just the command, but the moment the command arrives in.

The Four Types

One stated. Three inferred.

Intent isn't one thing. It arrives on four channels — some explicit, most not. A system that only hears the explicit one is fluent in commands and illiterate in everything else.

Type 1

Explicit Intent

The user states what they want. Clear, direct, often in the form of a command or question.

Example "Call Mom."   /   "Set a timer for 20 minutes."
The user voices the goal. AI just has to execute — no inference required.
Type 2

Active Intent

The user doesn't say what they want, but their current action reveals it.

Example You pick up your phone at 6:42am in running clothes with headphones in. You haven't asked anything. Intent: "I'm about to run — give me weather, route, and my playlist."
Type 3

Passive Intent

The user has a pattern. Tonight's moment matches the pattern, so the AI knows what usually follows.

Example It's 7pm on a Friday. Every Friday for the past three months you've ordered pizza. You haven't opened the app. Intent: "Probably pizza. Ask before acting."
Type 4

Ambient Intent

A continuous background signal. The user isn't asking for anything specific — they're just in a state that the environment should respect.

Example You're reading in bed at 11pm. Intent: "Stay calm. Keep the room warm. No notifications. Dim anything that isn't the book."

Most of today's AI only hears Type 1. The other three types exist — you experience them as the nudges you wish your phone would make but it never does. Context Grammar makes all four addressable.

Intent vs Jobs-to-be-Done

A framework a designer might confuse Intent with.

If you've worked in product, Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) is probably on your shelf. It's useful — but it is not what Intent is doing. The two sit at different timescales and answer different questions.

Framework

Jobs-to-be-Done

A durable goal the user hires a product to accomplish. Defined once, valid for months or years.

  • "Help me feel organized as a parent."
  • "Help me stay fit without scheduling workouts."
  • Drives product strategy — which problems to serve.
  • Lives in roadmaps, research decks, annual planning.
Context Grammar

Intent

A momentary signal of what the user wants right now. Revised every minute, every tap, every room-change.

  • "Pizza, probably. Ask me."
  • "Running in 5. Don't bother me with email."
  • Drives interaction execution — what AI should do this second.
  • Lives in the runtime, between the user and the rule engine.

JTBD tells you what product to build. Intent tells you what the product should do in the next 200 milliseconds. You need both. One without the other gives you either a well-researched product that is constantly annoying, or a responsive AI that is solving the wrong problem.

Intent × Brain

Intent without memory is just a guess.

The signal "you picked up your phone in running clothes" is only meaningful if AI remembers that you run. Intent and Brain aren't separate floors operating independently — they intersect. Floor 1 produces the signal; Floor 3 (Brain) supplies the context that makes the signal interpretable.
