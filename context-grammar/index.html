Context Grammar

A Design Language for the Age of Agentic AI

Your phone can write emails, book flights, summarize meetings. But it has no idea you are exhausted. Context Grammar gives AI the language to understand human state — and respond with intelligence, not just information.

Where It Starts

Intent has four depths.
AI only hears the loudest one.

When you set a 7am alarm, you said it out loud — Explicit. When you search for restaurants, you're Active without explaining why. Your daily commute pattern is Passive intent. A dark, still bedroom at midnight is Ambient. Context Grammar reads all four.

01
Explicit
Declared in words. "Wake me at 7am." "Order the usual." The most visible layer — and the least common in practice.
"Set an alarm for 7am"
02
Active
Revealed through action. Opening an app, searching, tapping. Intent without words — but clearly deliberate.
Opening the maps app near dinnertime
03
Passive
Embedded in habit. The 7am alarm you've set every weekday for three years. Pattern as preference.
Alarm set at 7am every weekday for 3 years
04
Ambient
Spoken by the environment. A dark bedroom. A stopped phone. A quiet house. Context that whispers what no one said.
Phone still, room dark, 11:47pm

The Problem

The Problem No One Named

AI assistants are brilliant at processing requests. They are blind to the person making them.

01 — Observation

AI can do incredible things

It writes code, generates images, plans trips, manages calendars. Every month the capabilities list gets longer. The technology works.

02 — Mystery

But it responds the same way every time

Whether you are relaxed or rushing. Alone or with your boss in the room. Browsing casually or making a decision that matters. Same input, same output. Why?

03 — Discovery

There is no shared language for human context

Designers say “the user is stressed.” Engineers ask “what is the data point for stressed?” Nobody has an answer. The conceptual layer is missing.

Context Grammar is that missing layer. Eight tokens that translate human state into structured signals AI can read, reason about, and act on.

Intent

What the user wants

Explicit or implicit. Sometimes the user states it directly. Sometimes the system infers it from the intersection of tokens and memory. Intent is the starting signal.

Deep dive →

8 Context Tokens

Six situation tokens. Two relationship dials.

Physical State. Cognitive Load. Social Exposure. Priority Weight. Form Factor. Feasibility. Plus two dials that govern the relationship: Autonomy Dial and Disclosure Dial. Together they describe who you are right now.

Deep dive →

Context Brain

Three layers of memory

Identity holds who you are. Accumulated Learning holds what the system has observed over time. Right Now holds what is true this moment. Memory is not unique to Context Grammar — the structured design language on top of it is.

Deep dive →

Rule Engine

Context becomes design rules

Tokens and memory feed into a rule engine that produces concrete design decisions. What to show, what to hide, how much autonomy to grant, which modality to use.

Deep dive →

Response

Three directions of adaptive behavior

Every response falls into one of three categories. Delegation — the system handles it. Escalation — the system asks for human input. Adaptation — the system adjusts its presentation. These are not abstractions. They map directly to AX patterns you can implement.
