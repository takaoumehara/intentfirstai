Context Grammar

A Design Language for the Age of Agentic AI

Your phone can write emails, book flights, summarize meetings. But it has no idea you are exhausted. Context Grammar gives AI the language to understand human state — and respond with intelligence, not just information.

Where It Starts

Intent has four depths.
AI only hears the loudest one.

When you set a 7am alarm, you said it out loud — Explicit. When you search for restaurants, you're Active without explaining why. Your daily commute pattern is Passive intent. A dark, still bedroom at midnight is Ambient. Context Grammar reads all four.

01
Explicit
Declared in words. "Wake me at 7am." "Order the usual." The most visible layer — and the least common in practice.
"Set an alarm for 7am"
02
Active
Revealed through action. Opening an app, searching, tapping. Intent without words — but clearly deliberate.
Opening the maps app near dinnertime
03
Passive
Embedded in habit. The 7am alarm you've set every weekday for three years. Pattern as preference.
Alarm set at 7am every weekday for 3 years
04
Ambient
Spoken by the environment. A dark bedroom. A stopped phone. A quiet house. Context that whispers what no one said.
Phone still, room dark, 11:47pm

The Problem

The Problem No One Named

AI assistants are brilliant at processing requests. They are blind to the person making them.

01 — Observation

AI can do incredible things

It writes code, generates images, plans trips, manages calendars. Every month the capabilities list gets longer. The technology works.

02 — Mystery

But it responds the same way every time

Whether you are relaxed or rushing. Alone or with your boss in the room. Browsing casually or making a decision that matters. Same input, same output. Why?

03 — Discovery

There is no shared language for human context

Designers say “the user is stressed.” Engineers ask “what is the data point for stressed?” Nobody has an answer. The conceptual layer is missing.

Context Grammar is that missing layer. Eight tokens that translate human state into structured signals AI can read, reason about, and act on.

Intent

What the user wants

Explicit or implicit. Sometimes the user states it directly. Sometimes the system infers it from the intersection of tokens and memory. Intent is the starting signal.

8 Context Tokens

Six situation tokens. Two relationship dials.

Physical State. Cognitive Load. Social Exposure. Priority Weight. Form Factor. Feasibility. Plus two dials that govern the relationship: Autonomy Dial and Disclosure Dial. Together they describe who you are right now.

Deep dive →

Context Brain

Three layers of memory

Identity holds who you are. Accumulated Learning holds what the system has observed over time. Right Now holds what is true this moment. Memory is not unique to Context Grammar — the structured design language on top of it is.

Deep dive →

Rule Engine

Context becomes design rules

Tokens and memory feed into a rule engine that produces concrete design decisions. What to show, what to hide, how much autonomy to grant, which modality to use.

Deep dive →

Response

Three directions of adaptive behavior

Every response falls into one of three categories. Delegation — the system handles it. Escalation — the system asks for human input. Adaptation — the system adjusts its presentation. These are not abstractions. They map directly to AX patterns you can implement.

Part 1
Input Grammar — Reading the Situation
8 Context Tokens + Context Brain + 2 Dials
Part 1 / Input Grammar

8 variables that tell AI
what kind of moment this is.

Six situation sensors read the environment. Two relationship dials control how far AI can reach. Together, they give AI the instinct to read the room.

Situation Tokens — 6
Environmental sensors. Read from device data, calendar, location, and behavior patterns.
①
Physical State
Walking, driving, still? Body position and motion signal what kind of response is even possible.
Waiter notices you're eating standing at the bar — keeps suggestions brief
②
Cognitive Load
Deep in a negotiation or relaxing on the couch? Estimated from time, calendar density, and recent behavior.
Waiter reads the table — intense business meeting gets minimal interruption
③
Social Exposure
Alone, with family, with a client? Who's in the room determines what can be shown.
Waiter doesn't read out wine prices when guests are visiting
④
Priority Weight
When two needs collide, which wins? Not just urgency — learned tradeoff patterns from the Brain.
Waiter knows this table always orders dessert before the check — sequences accordingly
⑤
Form Factor
Phone, TV, car dashboard, smart speaker? Each surface has different interaction constraints.
Waiter adjusts formality — different tone at the bar vs. the dining room
⑥
Feasibility
Can this actually be done right now? Technical and physical constraints in the moment.
Waiter doesn't suggest the sea bass when it ran out at lunch
Relationship Dials — 2
Not sensors — controls. How much to delegate. How much to disclose. Set by you, adjusted by AI over time.
⑦
Autonomy Dial
How far can AI act without asking? Four stages: Suggest → Confirm → Notify → Auto. Driven by three forces: service default, AI track record, and your active choice.
How much the restaurant can do without asking — from "show me a menu" to "just bring what you usually bring"
⑧
Disclosure Dial
How much does AI know about you? Full → Summary → Existence → Hidden. Works in two directions: protective (what AI can see) and connective (what AI bridges between people).
What you tell your regular waiter about your life — and what they tactfully pass to others at the table
Prerequisite Constraint Disclosure ≥ Autonomy. You cannot delegate what you haven't disclosed. "Handle everything" with "tell AI nothing" is logically impossible — like asking a waiter to order for you when they don't know your allergies.

Part 2 / Output Grammar

Every AI response
flows in one of three directions.

Once AI understands the situation through 8 tokens and the Brain, the Rule Engine transforms that understanding into a response direction. Three directions. Infinite variations.

→
Human → AI
Delegation
The user hands off a task. AI acts on their behalf — slowly building trust, always reversible.
"Order the usual" — the regular who trusts their waiter's judgment
←
AI → Human
Escalation
AI hits a boundary and returns control to the human. Fast, respectful of limits — not a failure.
"This dish is quite spicy — shall I confirm?" — the waiter who checks before assuming
↺
Context → UI
Adaptation
The environment changes, so the interface changes — automatically, instantly, fully reversible.
Kids arrive at the table → kids menu appears. No one asks.

Temporal Arc

Trust builds over months.
Breaks in seconds.

The Autonomy Dial doesn't jump from Suggest to Auto overnight. It climbs a staircase — Encounter → Learning → Maturation → Ambient. But one serious mistake sends it down an elevator. The asymmetry is intentional: trust earned slowly, trust lost instantly.
